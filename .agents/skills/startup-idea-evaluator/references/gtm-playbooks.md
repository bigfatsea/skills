# GTM Playbooks by Business Archetype

Reference for `startup-idea-evaluator`. Pick one primary archetype. Adapt concretely — never combine everything.

**Team context default**: ≤3 people, limited marketing budget, China+global access, AI tools available. Each archetype includes a "For this team" note.

---

## Archetype A: B2B SaaS — SMB

**Profile**: Low-to-mid ACV (<$10K/year), self-serve or light-touch sales, broad market.

**Beachhead**: One vertical niche. Become "the X for [specific industry]." Vertical focus compounds: referrals, case studies, and product insights reinforce each other.

**Channel priority**: Content SEO > founder-led outreach > product-led referral > niche community

**Early tactic**: Offer free pilots to 10 ideal customers in one vertical. Obsess over activation (aha moment). Get 3 named case studies before scaling.

**Key metric**: Activation rate (% reaching aha moment within 7 days)

**90-day goal**: 10 paying customers, >80% weekly active usage

**Don't do yet**: Paid ads, hiring salespeople, enterprise features, second vertical

**For this team**: Founder-led sales is feasible — cold email 50 precise ICPs, convert 3-5 to pilots. AI tools can accelerate: content drafting, email personalization, usage analytics. Avoid hiring a salesperson until pipeline exceeds what founders can handle.

---

## Archetype B: B2B SaaS — Enterprise

**Profile**: High ACV (>$50K/year), custom deal, long cycle, multi-stakeholder.

**Beachhead**: One lighthouse customer. Their logo and quote is your first GTM asset.

**Channel priority**: Warm intros > strategic outbound > industry conferences

**Early tactic**: "Design partner" program — co-build with 2-3 enterprises in exchange for a paid contract and reference rights. Validates roadmap and builds trust simultaneously.

**Key metric**: Pipeline value, sales cycle length, decision-maker access rate

**90-day goal**: 2-3 signed design partners, 10+ qualified conversations

**Don't do yet**: PLG motion, freemium, broad marketing, second ICP before first is proven

**For this team**: Enterprise sales with a 3-person team is possible via warm intros from existing network. AI tools help with contract review, demo prep, proposal drafting. The key constraint is trust — one founder must be able to credibly engage C-suite. If the team lacks enterprise relationships, reconsider this archetype or find an advisor with access.

**China note**: Enterprise sales in China often requires WeChat relationship maintenance, attendance at industry events, and sometimes government connections. Factor this into the time budget.

## Archetype C: Consumer App

**Profile**: Mass market, free or low-cost, retention-driven, viral potential.

**Beachhead**: Find the "atomic community" — smallest group where the product is genuinely 10x better. Don't start broad.

**Channel priority**: Community seeding > micro-KOC > organic content > platform-native growth

**Early tactic**: Unscalable manual onboarding. DM 200 potential users in target community. Create content that attracts the specific tribe. Be present where they already hang out.

**Key metric**: D7 retention (% still active 7 days after first use)

**90-day goal**: 1,000 retained users with organic word-of-mouth signal (K-factor > 0.3)

**Don't do yet**: Paid UA, app store optimization, gamification, broad feature expansion

**For this team**: Consumer is the hardest archetype for a small team with limited marketing, because distribution is the product. Proceed only if: (a) the founders ARE the target users and have natural access to the community, or (b) there's a clear viral mechanic built into the product itself.

**China channels**: WeChat mini-programs and group sharing; Xiaohongshu KOC seeding (more cost-effective than KOL); Douyin short-form content if product has visual appeal; WeChat group water-drop-style community growth.

**International channels**: Niche Reddit/Discord communities; TikTok/Instagram short demos; targeted Twitter/X thread marketing; Product Hunt for early adopter apps.

## Archetype D: Marketplace / Platform

**Profile**: Two-sided network, liquidity is the product, chicken-and-egg problem.

**Beachhead**: One geography or one vertical. Prove liquidity there before expanding. Most marketplaces die from premature geographic spread.

**Chicken-and-egg options**:
- Single-player mode (value even without the other side)
- Fake the supply side early (aggregated listings, white-label providers)
- Subsidize or own one side temporarily
- Restrict supply to create scarcity that attracts demand

**Channel priority**: Supply-side manual outreach first > demand-side content > cross-listing on existing platforms

**Early tactic**: Concierge marketplace — manually match supply and demand. Understand the transaction before automating it.

**Key metric**: Liquidity rate (% of listings/requests that transact within X days)

**90-day goal**: 100+ completed transactions in one geo/vertical with proven unit economics

**Don't do yet**: Geographic expansion, full automation, bilateral marketing simultaneously

**For this team**: The manual concierge phase is actually well-suited for a small team — it requires judgment and hustle, not a marketing machine. AI can help with: matching logic drafts, communication templates, supplier onboarding. The key question is: can this team manually service the beachhead while also building the platform? If not, reduce the scope further.

## Archetype E: DevTool / API / Infrastructure

**Profile**: Developers as primary users, bottoms-up adoption, community-driven.

**Beachhead**: 3-5 respected engineers/teams who become vocal advocates. Credibility travels fast in dev communities.

**Channel priority**: Documentation quality > dev content (tutorials, blog, open source) > Hacker News / GitHub / Discord / Reddit

**Early tactic**: Build the best documentation in your category — this is product, not marketing. Offer white-glove integration support for first 20 customers. Give it away free to get real usage data.

**Key metric**: API calls or active integrations per account (depth of usage, not signup count)

**90-day goal**: 50 integrated teams, 5 vocal advocates, active community channel

**Don't do yet**: Enterprise packaging, partner programs, premium features before core reliability

**For this team**: This is one of the best archetypes for a small, technical, AI-savvy team. The team can: write excellent docs with AI assistance, contribute to adjacent open-source projects to gain credibility, ship fast due to small decision overhead. A JS/Python stack is ideal here. Consider open-sourcing the core to build community trust and inbound leads.

## Archetype F: AI Vertical SaaS

**Profile**: AI delivers measurable efficiency/quality improvement in a specific workflow.

**Beachhead**: Single workflow where AI improvement is most measurable. "Before/after" framing must be demonstrable in 10 minutes with a customer's own data.

**Channel priority**: Niche industry communities > targeted content (ROI case studies) > partner channel with vertical software

**Early tactic**: Run a real demo with the customer's actual data. Quantify time/cost savings in dollars and hours. The ROI story must be undeniable to spread internally.

**Key metric**: Time-to-value (how fast a new user experiences the AI benefit), then expansion MRR

**90-day goal**: 20 paying customers with documented, shareable ROI stories

**Don't do yet**: Horizontal platform expansion, adjacent verticals, custom model training before PMF

**For this team**: This is the strongest archetype match. A 3-person AI-augmented team can: build AI features rapidly with openrouter.ai + JS/Python, iterate fast on prompts and workflows, serve early customers with high-touch support. Core risk: AI commodity risk — if the underlying model capability becomes a commodity, what remains defensible? Prioritize workflow integration depth and proprietary data loops as moat.

**China note**: Many Chinese enterprise buyers are actively seeking AI efficiency tools. Government endorsement or pilot programs (if applicable) can unlock budget. Frame in terms of cost reduction (降本增效).

## Archetype G: Hardware + Software / IoT

**Profile**: Physical product with embedded software, supply chain complexity, high capital requirements.

**Beachhead**: Start with software-only or minimal-hardware version if at all possible. Delay manufacturing as long as possible.

**Channel priority**: Pre-orders/crowdfunding > early adopter communities > distribution partnerships

**Early tactic**: Validate demand before committing to manufacturing. Get LOIs or pre-orders with real money. Use rough prototypes for feedback.

**Key metric**: Pre-order conversion rate, NPS from prototype users

**90-day goal**: Validated demand signal (LOIs or pre-orders), working prototype with 20+ real users

**Don't do yet**: Mass manufacturing, retail distribution, full feature set, second hardware SKU

**For this team**: Hardware adds supply chain risk that a small team struggles to absorb. A 3-person team should aggressively delay the hardware component: use off-the-shelf hardware + custom software first; partner with a CM early to understand true BOM costs; don't commit to manufacturing until software is working and demand is proven. If hardware is truly core, seek a hardware-experienced advisor or co-founder before proceeding.

## Cross-Cutting Rules

1. **One channel to master first.** Multi-channel at day 1 = mediocre everywhere. Especially true for a small team.
2. **Do unscalable things first.** Manual processes reveal what to automate and what customers actually value.
3. **One north-star metric per phase.** If you're tracking more than one KPI as "the most important thing," you're not prioritized.
4. **Speed of learning > speed of growth** (pre-PMF). Optimize feedback loops, not vanity metrics.
5. **Re-evaluate GTM every 30 days.** What works at 10 customers rarely works at 100.
6. **First customers are co-creators.** Give them your personal contact. Treat them as design partners.
7. **The "don't do yet" list matters as much as the "do" list.** Saying no to everything except the one right thing is a skill most founders lack — especially hard for small teams handling everything.

## GTM Motion Selection

Pick one primary motion. A small team with limited marketing should default toward PLG or CLG where possible.

| Motion | Best for | 3-person team fit | Primary metric |
|--------|----------|-------------------|----------------|
| **PLG** (Product-led) | Low ACV, self-serve, viral potential | ★★★★★ — product does the selling | Activation rate, expansion MRR |
| **SLG** (Sales-led) | High ACV, complex enterprise | ★★★ — viable via founder-led sales, not SDR team | Pipeline velocity, win rate |
| **CLG** (Community-led) | DevTools, professionals, trust-dependent | ★★★★ — suits technical founders | Community engagement, advocacy |
| **BLG** (Brand/content) | Consumer, media-friendly | ★★ — requires consistent content output | Inbound traffic, brand search |
| **PLS** (Partner-led) | Regulated, distribution-constrained | ★★ — needs strong partner relationships | Partner-sourced revenue % |

## China / International Dual-Market Tactics

**For B2C (China)**:
- WeChat: mini-programs, official account, group seeding, moments organic
- Xiaohongshu: KOC (micro-influencer) seeding, <5K followers with high engagement is often better than big KOL
- Douyin: short-form product demos, challenge formats
- 私域流量 (owned traffic pools): core asset — once acquired, keep users in WeChat groups

**For B2B (China)**:
- Industry WeChat groups and vertical communities
- DingTalk / Feishu / WeCom ecosystem integration as distribution wedge
- Industry conferences and local meetups for enterprise relationships
- 口碑+案例 (word of mouth + case studies) are the primary enterprise sales tools

**For International**:
- Developer/technical audience: HN, GitHub, Discord, Product Hunt
- B2B SaaS: cold email to precise ICP (100 emails, not 10,000), LinkedIn outreach for enterprise
- B2C: targeted subreddits, niche communities, TikTok/Instagram, Product Hunt
- Content SEO: compounds over time, works while the team sleeps — ideal for small teams

**Dual-market sequencing**: Usually better to fully prove one market first before expanding to the other. Exception: if the product has genuine network effects across both markets, or if international market has better pricing/margins (common for SaaS).
