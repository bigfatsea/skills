# Interview Execution & Follow-Up

Reference for SKILL.md advisory role during or after interviews. Read this when advising on how to conduct an interview, manage special situations, or plan follow-up sessions.

---

## C. Interview Execution

### C.1 Pre-Recording Checklist (30 Minutes Before)

- [ ] Check equipment battery, storage capacity, clear old recordings
- [ ] Test recording level (normal speaking distance, peak at -6 dB to -12 dB)
- [ ] Choose a quiet location; close doors, windows, A/C; silence mobile phones
- [ ] Prepare paper outline + pen (not digital — paper allows margin notes without losing eye contact)
- [ ] Prepare water and tissues (emotional response contingency)
- [ ] Prepare consent documents

### C.2 Recording Lead-In (Speak Into Device)

Before beginning substantive content, record the following on-track:

> "Today is [date, day/month/year]. Interview with [narrator name or pseudonym], interviewed by [interviewer name]. Location: [general description — e.g., 'narrator's home', not the specific address]. Topic: [this session's topic]. Estimated session length: [X] minutes. Start time: [HH:MM]."

This creates a permanent record for archival and later transcription context. (OAH Best Practices requirement)

### C.3 On-Site Execution Rules

| Rule | What NOT to do | What TO do |
|---|---|---|
| Use "what" not "why" | "Why did you leave that company?" | "What was happening when you left that company?" |
| Use "how" not "was it" | "Were you sad?" | "What was going through your mind at that point?" |
| Let silence exist | Fill silence immediately | Wait 5–10 seconds before intervening |
| Don't interrupt | "Hold on, let me finish that question" | Let them finish; then say "I'd like to come back to what you said about X" |
| Don't project | "I've had similar experiences..." | Keep focus on the narrator |
| Don't lead | "So you're saying it was unfair?" | "I want to make sure I understood — I heard X. Is that right?" |
| Handle avoidance | Immediately drop the topic | "You mentioned X a moment ago — I'm curious about that" (try once; if avoided, mark and move on) |

### C.3.A Active Listening — The Foundation

Effective interviewing is **80% listening, 20% questioning.** The interviewer's job is not to conduct an interrogation but to create conditions in which the narrator tells their own story.

Active listening has three components (Rogers & Farson):

| Component | What it means | In practice |
|---|---|---|
| **Comprehension** | Understand what is being communicated, not just the literal words | Ask yourself: "What is the narrator actually trying to tell me?" |
| **Retaining** | Hold what is said across the session without interrupting to record | Use margin notes on your paper outline; do not stop eye contact |
| **Responding** | Signal that you heard and understood — three response types below | |

**Three response types:**
- **Paraphrase:** Restate in your own words. "So if I'm hearing you right, after the factory closed you had about two years of real uncertainty." → Narrator confirms or corrects.
- **Reflect feeling:** Name the emotional register you observe. "It sounds like that period was genuinely frightening." Do NOT say "I understand how you feel" — it redirects to the interviewer.
- **Clarify:** Ask about the specific ambiguity. "When you say 'everything changed,' what changed first?" — opens rather than closes.

The **S.O.C.I.A.L.** framework below operationalizes these three response types in a live interview sequence.

### C.3.B Cross-Cultural & Generational Considerations

| Context | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| **Elder narrators (70+)** | Slower pace; allow tangential stories (they often circle back); do not correct dates; avoid era-specific vocabulary that may carry political weight |
| **Young interviewer / elder narrator** | Adopt explicit learner posture: "I wasn't alive then — help me understand what that felt like." Avoid projecting your own reference points |
| **Dialect speakers** | If narrator prefers their regional dialect, encourage it; transcription difficulty is worth the authenticity gain; dialect comfort correlates with narrative depth |
| **Narrator defers to interviewer (power distance)** | Self-censor risk: redirect firmly — "I'm here to hear your memory. There's no right answer." Reduce formality; avoid note-taking theatrics |
| **Narrator holds authority (senior / public figure)** | May perform rather than remember; use informal, specific, and personal questions to break the public-facing mode |

### C.4 The S.O.C.I.A.L. Follow-Up Framework

Use this sequence when a response warrants deeper exploration:

| Step | Action | Best timing |
|---|---|---|
| **S**ilence | Wait 5–10 seconds; give space | After any answer; after sensitive topics |
| **O**bserve | Note nonverbal cues: expression, posture, gaze, hand position | Throughout |
| **C**larify | Pin down vague references: "When you say 'that time', what period do you mean?" | At any ambiguous phrase |
| **I**nquire | Targeted probe: "Can you say more about why you made that decision?" | When a key moment has just surfaced |
| **A**bstract-Concrete | Switch levels: "Overall, how was that period for you?" / "Give me a specific moment." | After a topic has been introduced |
| **L**ink | Connect threads: "How does that connect to what you told me earlier about X?" | When thematic connections appear |

### C.5 Dynamic Outline Adjustment (Note in Paper Outline Margins)

| Signal | Action |
|---|---|
| Narrator visibly energized / talking freely about a topic | Add follow-up depth; merge subsequent planned questions here |
| Narrator visibly avoiding a topic | Mark [deferred]; do not push; attempt again in follow-up session |
| Narrator mentions something entirely unplanned | Immediately write "follow-up on [X]" in margin |
| A prepared question is now inapplicable | Cross it out; note "why not asked" at bottom of outline |
| Signs of fatigue (60+ minutes) | Begin closing sequence; keep 10 minutes for open-ended catch-all |

**Talese's rule:** "Never interrupt even when people are having great difficulty explaining themselves, for during such halting and imprecise moments... people are very revealing. What they hesitate to talk about can tell much about them. Their pauses, their evasions, their sudden shifts in subject matter are likely indicators of what embarrasses them, or irritates them, or what they regard as too private." (Gay Talese, *Origins of a Nonfiction Writer*)

→ Silence, hesitation, avoidance, and topic-shifts are data, not failures.

### C.6 Special Scenario Handling

| Scenario | Response |
|---|---|
| Narrator cries | Pause; offer tissues; wait for narrator to signal readiness to continue. Do NOT say "it's okay" or "don't worry." Instead: "Would you like to take a break?" |
| Narrator becomes angry | Do not evaluate or interrupt; acknowledge the emotion: "It sounds like that period was very significant for you." |
| Narrator refuses to answer | "It's completely fine not to go there. We can move to something else or stop for today." |
| Narrator goes off-topic | Let them finish; then: "Let me gather together the main points you just raised." (This is often the most revealing material — do not cut it off.) |
| Narrator says "I don't know" | "I don't know" is itself information: "That you're not sure — what does that bring to mind?" |
| Third party enters (family member, colleague) | Politely suggest they wait elsewhere; if they insist, note on record that a third party was present; do not incorporate their contributions as the narrator's testimony |
| Equipment failure | Stop recording immediately; note the timestamp; explain to narrator; capture what you can from memory in interviewer notes immediately after |

### C.7 Post-Recording Immediate Actions (Within 5 Minutes)

- [ ] Back up recording to 2 separate locations (local + cloud)
- [ ] Write **interviewer notes**: your emotional response to the session, your own hunches and judgments, follow-up threads you noticed, anything that did NOT make it into the recording
- [ ] Mark up your paper outline: which questions were asked in which order, what was skipped and why

**Why interviewer notes matter:** These are kept separate from the transcript and represent your subjective view as the interviewer — not the narrator's words. They are an independent layer of the archive. (OAH Best Practices: "the 'cost' of a project is more than just financial [...] the emotional labor involved for the interviewer, project manager, and/or team members.")

## D. Follow-Up Interview

### D.1 Follow-Up Trigger Table

| Trigger | Example | When to assess |
|---|---|---|
| New lead discovered | "My old mentor — he later went to X..." (mentioned but not explored) | Day of interview, during review |
| Contradiction found | Same event described differently in different sessions | During transcription |
| Third-party verification needed | A critical event involving someone not yet interviewed | During analysis |
| Narrator proactively follows up | "I've been thinking — there's something else I should have said..." | Respond immediately; schedule follow-up |
| Narrator's attitude changes | Initially avoided a topic; later makes contact voluntarily | Assess case-by-case |
| Gap found during organization | Listening back reveals a key question was never asked | During transcript organization |

**Principle:** "No project without a follow-up is complete — 90% of the best material surfaces in follow-ups."

### D.2 Follow-Up Techniques

- More targeted than the first session — no need for extensive rapport-warming
- Open with: "Last time you mentioned X. I've been curious about Y." — This shows the narrator their words were remembered and valued
- Avoid interrogation tone — keep pace lighter; session length can be shorter (30–45 minutes)
- Consider bringing notes from the first session to show — narrators appreciate seeing their words treated carefully
- Do not repeat questions already answered well; focus only on unresolved threads

**Key distinction:**
- **Follow-up question** (in-session): deeper probe of what was just said — belongs in Mode 1 execution
- **Follow-up interview** (new session): separate meeting to fill gaps, resolve contradictions, explore new leads — belongs in project lifecycle management

### D.3 Follow-Up Pre-Checklist

- [ ] Is there a specific, defined goal for this follow-up? (Avoid vague "let's chat more" sessions)
- [ ] Has the narrator agreed to meet again (vs. being passively invited)?
- [ ] Is the time gap appropriate? (Typically 2–4 weeks after first session)
- [ ] Has a new, simplified outline been prepared? (Usually shorter than the original)

## H. Interviewer Growth

**Corrective:** Interviewing is not "asking questions." It is listening so skillfully that the narrator feels safe enough to tell the truth.

- **After every session:** Write interviewer notes within 5 minutes (§C.7). First impressions decay in under 30 minutes — do not trust memory.
- **Quarterly self-review:** Listen back to 2–3 of your own recordings. Ask: What do I habitually over-ask? What topics do I avoid? Do I fill silences too quickly?
- **Peer review:** Share one session with a trusted peer and invite criticism, not praise. Blind spots are invisible by definition.
- **Study your transcripts:** Note where you interrupted, projected, or led. No interviewer is exempt from these habits.
- **Emotional self-care:** Oral history interviewing is emotional labor (OAH). Processing narratives of displacement, grief, or trauma has a cumulative effect on the interviewer. Name the weight; do not absorb it silently.
