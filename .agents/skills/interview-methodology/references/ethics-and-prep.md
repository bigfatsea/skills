# Ethics & Pre-Interview Preparation

Detailed reference for SKILL.md Mode 1. Read this when generating a pre-interview research report, interview guide, or ethics checklist.

---

## A. Ethics & Legal Framework

### A.1 The Six-Step Consent Process

| Step | Action | Document | Timing |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Prepare project description (purpose, scope, intended use, risks) | Project description sheet | Before first contact |
| 2 | Prepare recording consent form (right to withdraw, pseudonym, restricted access) | Recording consent form | Before first contact |
| 3 | Prepare release form (rights assignment, publication scope, copyright) | Release form | Before first contact |
| 4 | Walk through all documents at pre-interview meeting; give narrator reading time | Verbal + documents | Pre-interview meeting |
| 5 | Verbal on-record consent at start of recording | On recording | Opening of every session |
| 6 | Sign release form after interview is complete | Release form signed | Post-interview |

**Why step 5 before step 6:** Many oral historians prefer obtaining the legal release form signature *after* the interview, so that any sensitive content that arose can be addressed in the release terms. But recording consent must be obtained *before* the recording begins. (OAH Best Practices)

### A.2 Narrator's Three Rights — Must Be Stated Before Recording

1. **Right to withdraw** — narrator may refuse to discuss any topic at any time
2. **Right to use a pseudonym** — narrator may choose to be identified by a different name
3. **Right to restrict access** — narrator may request that part or all of the interview be restricted from public access

These are not optional formalities. Fail to state them, and you have not obtained valid informed consent.

### A.3 Handling Sensitive or Monitored Communities

When the interview involves a subject from a community that is politically, legally, or socially vulnerable: collect only the minimum necessary background information; do not record identifying information that could create risk. (OAH Best Practices)

### A.4 Narrator Subjectivity — A Feature, Not a Flaw

Oral sources tell us less about events than about their *meaning*. The unique element oral history forces upon the historian is the speaker's subjectivity. (Alessandro Portelli)

Different versions of the same event across sessions are not dishonesty — they are *narrative reconstruction*. Mark contradictions with timestamps; do not reconcile them into a single authoritative version. Pursue the narrator's truth, not an objective "what really happened."

### A.5 Legal Status Note

Oral interviews are legally serious documents. The transcript/manuscript copyright typically rests with the interviewer or project (subject to contract); the narrator's factual statements are generally not independently copyrightable. Third-party mentions (especially negative portrayals) may create defamation or privacy liability — flag before publication.

### A.6 Ethics Self-Check (Run Before Every Recording Session)

- [ ] Does the narrator fully understand the project purpose?
- [ ] Does the narrator know how the recording will be used?
- [ ] Does the narrator know they can withdraw, use a pseudonym, or restrict access at any time?
- [ ] Am I pursuing the narrator's truth, not an objective "what really happened"?
- [ ] Am I guarding against imposing my own interpretation?
- [ ] Am I prepared to give the narrator appropriate respect (no interrupting, no judging)?
- [ ] Do I have a mechanism to manage my own emotional responses (interviewer notes)?

## B. Pre-Interview Preparation

### B.1 Interviewer Training Checklist

Before conducting a first interview, the interviewer should have basic training in:

- [ ] Informed consent process (steps A.1–A.3 above)
- [ ] Recording equipment operation + backup procedure
- [ ] Active listening techniques
- [ ] Follow-up question techniques
- [ ] Handling sensitive topics (trauma, religion, politics, death)
- [ ] Transcription / organization basics

### B.2 Two Pre-Interview Meetings Protocol

**Meeting 1 — Project Introduction:**
- Self-introduction + project background
- Explain recording process, session length, intended use
- Explain narrator's rights (withdraw / pseudonym / restricted access)
- Obtain initial (verbal) consent
- Ask what the narrator hopes to contribute or accomplish

**Meeting 2 — Preview & Expectations:**
- Discuss outline *topic clusters* (not specific questions)
- Confirm "must-cover" and "off-limits" topics
- Ask preferred form of address
- Learn about narrator's accent, dialect, or speech patterns (for transcription calibration)
- Confirm date, location, equipment

**Purpose of both meetings:** "These meetings are important in establishing rapport between interviewer and narrator." (OAH Best Practices) — Do not treat first contact as information extraction. Treat it as trust-building.

### B.3 Research Dimensions Table (Fill Before Interview)

| Dimension | What to find | Suggested sources |
|---|---|---|
| **Background** | Age, birthplace, family structure, education, occupation | Public records, social media |
| **Relationship map** | Key family members, friends, colleagues, rivals, mentors | Narrator's own accounts, public records |
| **Timeline** | Major life events, chronological | Autobiography, résumé, news archives |
| **Key events** | Known turning points (marriage, career shifts, trauma, achievements) | News coverage, social media |
| **Historical context** | Era, social and political backdrop of the narrator's life | Historical records, journalism |
| **Controversies** | Known disputes or incidents involving the narrator | Public records (handle carefully) |
| **Soft signals** | Catchphrases, hobbies, distinctive habits | Social media, word-of-mouth |
| **Legal/ethics flags** | Third-party involvements, unpublished information, open legal cases | Internal review |

### B.4 Outline Building Method

**Principle:** Start from topic clusters, not from pre-formed questions.

- Topic cluster = a domain of experience (e.g., "childhood", "career turning point", "relationship with X")
- Questions emerge from the topic; they are not the topic itself
- Use open-ended questions ("what", "how") — not yes/no or leading questions
- Annotate every question with its purpose: `[purpose: warm-up / scene-building / key event / reflection]`

**Structural rules:**
- Max 2 A4 pages
- Flexible time blocks — never fill 90 minutes end-to-end
- Reserve 15 minutes at the end for narrator to raise anything uncovered
- Prepare 5 contingency questions for cold/resistant moments
- Use paper outline during interview (not digital) — allows margin notes, maintains eye contact

**Four types of questions in every cluster:**

| Type | Example | Purpose |
|---|---|---|
| Scene-opener | "What's the earliest memory you have of that place?" | Warm-up, establishes sensory grounding |
| Relationship | "Who were the people around you at that time?" | Populates relationship map |
| Key event | "What happened that changed the direction of things?" | Finds turning points |
| Reflection | "Looking back now, how do you see that period?" | Surfaces meaning and theme |

### B.5 Sample Outline (Life Experience Type — Simplified)

```
Topic 1: Childhood  [15 min]
- What is your earliest memory?  [purpose: warm-up]
- Where did you grow up? What was the world like around you?  [purpose: scene-building]
- What were your parents / caregivers like in your childhood?  [purpose: relationship map]
- What moment in childhood had the most lasting effect on you?  [purpose: key event]

Topic 2: Education / Career Turning Point  [20 min]
- How did you come to choose [field / work]?  [purpose: decision point]
- When was the turning point, and why?  [purpose: key event]
- What was the biggest obstacle at that time?  [purpose: conflict / challenge]
- What did you learn from [that experience]?  [purpose: reflection / theme]

Topic 3: Core Relationship / Event  [20 min]
- [Based on known key relationship from pre-interview research]
- ...

Topic 4: Present & Future  [10 min]
- How do you see that period now, from where you stand today?  [purpose: retrospective meaning]
- If you could say one thing to your younger self, what would it be?  [purpose: inner theme]

Topic 5: Anything Else  [15 min]
- Is there anything important we haven't covered that you'd like to share?  [purpose: catch-all]

Contingency questions (cold / resistance):
1. Tell me about a typical day during that period.
2. Who was the most important person in that chapter of your life?
3. What's a moment you've thought about a lot over the years?
4. What did you want back then that you didn't have?
5. What would surprise people to know about you during that time?
```
