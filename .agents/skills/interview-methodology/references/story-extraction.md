# Story Core Extraction

Reference for SKILL.md Mode 3. Read this when identifying themes, mapping story arcs, assessing narrative angles, and producing a distillation for creative use.

---

## F.1 Three Foundational Narrative Concepts

These three concepts serve as analytical frameworks — not as prescriptions for what the story *must* look like.

| Concept | Definition | Application in nonfiction |
|---|---|---|
| **Theme** | The main topic, subject, or message of a narrative (Wikipedia) | Abstract to a word or short phrase: "growth", "belonging", "betrayal", "the weight of choice" |
| **Story Arc** | The chronological construction of plot (Wikipedia) | Find setup / confrontation / resolution — or the moments where these are disrupted |
| **Hero's Journey** | Joseph Campbell's 17-stage monomyth (The Hero with a Thousand Faces) | Use as a lens: call to adventure / refusal / crossing the threshold / trials / transformation / return |

**Important caveat:** These are analytical tools, not mandatory templates. Many powerful oral histories resist the hero's journey model. Use them to ask questions of the material, not to force the material into a shape.

---

## F.2 Five Theme Extraction Methods

Apply all five methods; the intersection of their results is where the real theme lives.

| Method | Operation | The question to ask |
|---|---|---|
| **Frequency** | Count which words, people, events, or concepts the narrator returns to across the session | "What 3–5 things come up again and again?" |
| **Emotional Intensity** | Mark where the narrator is most animated, most distressed, or most subdued | "Where is the emotional temperature highest or lowest?" |
| **Contrast** | Compare the narrator's attitude at the beginning vs. end of the session — or about the same event in different contexts | "What has changed between then and now, or between version A and version B?" |
| **Binary Opposition** | Identify recurring pairs of opposites in the narrative | "What tensions run through the story? (tradition/modernity, city/countryside, alone/belonging)" |
| **Omission** | Find what the narrator consistently does not say — the gaps, the avoidances, the topics that circle but never land | "What is not said? What is systematically absent?" |

**How to synthesize:** The theme candidate that shows up across the most methods is likely the real center. A theme that only appears in frequency but not in intensity may be habitual, not meaningful.

## F.3 Story Arc Identification

### The Three-Act Model (Classical)

| Act | Label | What to look for in the material |
|---|---|---|
| Act 1 | **Setup** | "Who was this person before the story begins?" |
| Act 2 | **Confrontation** | "What happened that forced a response, a change, a crisis?" |
| Act 3 | **Resolution** | "Who or what emerged from that? What was lost and gained?" |

### Key Structural Moments to Locate

- **Turning point:** The moment when the direction of the narrative changes — "and then everything was different"
- **Low point:** The moment of maximum pressure, loss, or failure — the bottom of the arc
- **Epiphany:** The moment of sudden recognition or changed understanding — "when I finally understood..."

### When the Arc Is Absent or Disrupted

Not all oral histories have clean arcs. Some possibilities:
- **Circular narrative:** Returns to the same place or feeling; the arc is a loop, not a line
- **Fragmented narrative:** Non-linear; the meaning is in the fragmentation itself
- **Unresolved narrative:** The story is still happening; there is no resolution yet

These are not failures. Note which structural pattern best describes the material, and flag it for creative decisions downstream.

## F.4 Six Principles of Narrative Writing (For Scene Identification)

When building the key scene list in Mode 3 output, apply these principles to identify what makes a scene worth reconstructing:

| Principle | What it means | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Scene over summary** | Show the specific moment, not the general pattern | Not: "He was often sad." But: "He held the envelope for three minutes without opening it." |
| **Direct quote** | The narrator's exact words outperform paraphrase | Not: "She said her father was strict." But: "She said: 'He never once told me I did something right.'" |
| **Detail accumulation** | One concrete detail outweighs 100 adjectives | Not: "The factory was old and run-down." But: "The windows hadn't been cleaned since 1981." |
| **Temporal precision** | Specific time grounds the reader | Not: "that autumn." But: "September 1987." |
| **Sensory grounding** | Include sound, smell, and touch — not just sight | Not: "The kitchen was warm." But: "The kitchen smelled of machine oil and dried chrysanthemum." |
| **Emotional restraint** | Let the detail carry the feeling — don't announce it | Not: "It was the most painful moment of her life." But: let the scene show it. |

## F.5 Six Narrative Angle Assessment Table

For each angle: mark **applicable** (strong material exists) / **marginal** (thin material) / **not applicable** (no real material).

| Angle | What it centers | Applicability question | One-sentence pitch format |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Struggle / Growth** | Transformation of the narrator over time, typically through effort and adversity | "Is there a clear before-and-after arc driven by struggle?" | "The story of [person] growing from [X] to [Y] through [what struggle]." |
| **Relationship / Bond** | A key relationship that defines or shapes the narrator | "Is there a relationship whose evolution drives the story?" | "The story of [person]'s relationship with [X], and how it [changed/defined them]." |
| **Conflict / Opposition** | A central antagonism or tension that runs through the story | "Is there a sustained tension that structures the narrative?" | "The story of [person] against [X], and what that cost / made possible." |
| **Era / Social Mirror** | The narrator's story as a window into a historical or social moment | "Does the personal story illuminate a larger context?" | "Through [person]'s experience, the story of [era/community/phenomenon]." |
| **Reversal / Epiphany** | A decisive moment that reorients the narrator's worldview | "Is there a single moment where everything shifted?" | "The story of the moment [person] understood [X], and what came after." |
| **Commitment / Sacrifice** | What the narrator held onto or gave up, and at what cost | "Is there a sustained choice or principle that runs through the narrative?" | "The story of what [person] refused to give up, and what that refusal cost." |

**Selection criteria (in order of priority):**
1. Where does the narrator's emotional intensity peak?
2. Which angle has the richest scene material (concrete, sensory, specific)?
3. Which angle best serves the project's intended output?
4. Which angle would create the strongest connection with the intended audience?

## F.6 One-Sentence Story Test

A story that can be stated clearly in one sentence is a story with a defined center. Use this to test the recommended angle.

**Template:**
```
[Person] — [in situation/context] — who experienced [turning point/challenge] — and ultimately [change/choice/what they carried forward].
```

**Examples:**
- ❌ Weak: "She's an engineer who later started a business." (Too vague — no tension, no stakes)
- ✅ Strong: "An engineer raised in 1980s northeastern China's factory system who, at 40, abandoned a state job to pursue AI entrepreneurship in Beijing — and what that cost her."

**Test it:** If someone who knows nothing about the narrator hears this sentence, do they want to hear the story? If yes, the angle works.

## F.7 Five-Question Story Test

Apply to the recommended angle before finalizing the extraction report.

| Question | Pass standard | What a "no" means |
|---|---|---|
| **Is this a story only this person could tell?** | Specific details, singular experience | The story is generic; needs more specificity |
| **Is this a story a stranger could understand?** | Universal emotional access point | The story is too internal; needs a bridge to the audience |
| **Is this a story worth telling?** | Some form of value: insight, beauty, witness, historical record | The story needs a clearer "why does this matter" |
| **Is this a credible story?** | Details are internally consistent; contradictions are acknowledged | Flag contradictions; do not paper over them |
| **Is this a complete story?** | Arc has some form of closure, even if open-ended | Flag if the arc is genuinely unresolved; note what would complete it |

A "no" on any question is not a disqualification — it is a flag for the creative team (and for follow-up interviews, if relevant).
